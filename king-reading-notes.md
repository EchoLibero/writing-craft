## MEMOIR (Lines 1–1100)

### Origin
- **Birth:** October 21, 1947, Portland, Maine
- **Father:** died when Stephen was 2 (hit by car, driver never identified)
- **Mother:** Ruth Pillsbury King — strong, worked multiple jobs, raised boys alone
- **Brother:** Dave (older, smarter, more restless)

### Key childhood moments
- First story sold to mother for $1 ("Больше денег за хорошую историю")
- **OSB (Ox-Sheep-Bird):** Boy = Ox, then Sheep, then Bird — social hierarchy, King was Bird
- Mother: "Write about yourself" — started writing, never stopped
- **"Деревенская отрыжка"** — satirical school newspaper, got in trouble, lesson: "don't satirize"
- English teacher Mr. Boucher told him he had talent but should write about "real" people, not horror

### Writing career
- **High school:** wrote stories for school literary magazine
- **College:** University of Maine, English literature
- **Teacher:** taught at Haskell High, writing classes
- **Tabitha (Tabby):** wife, saved first "Carrie" draft from trash
  - Said: "There's something good here. I can feel it."

### Carrie origin (lines 200–300)
- Idea: girls' shower, menstruation + telekinesis
- **Source:** Sondra (epilepsy, died alone), Dody (postpartum depression, suicide) — real girls from King's school
- First draft thrown away. Tabitha fished it out.
- Sold to Doubleday for $2,500 advance — ended up being about $200K after paperback rights sold to Signet for $400K

### Alcohol (1966–1989)
- Started: 1966, Washington DC trip
- Realization: garbage can full of 64oz Miller Lite in 3 days
- **"Hemingway Defense":** "Writers drink, it's part of the craft"
- Reality: addiction is addiction, creativity and substances don't go together
- **Recovery:** 1988, Tabitha staged intervention
- "Misery" was written during addiction; he barely remembers writing it

### The desk metaphor
- Big oak desk (ceremonial) → 6 years drinking/high at it
- After recovery: smaller desk, under the eaves, no hangover
- **"Life is not a support system for art. It's the other way around."**

### 1999 accident (June 19)
- Hit by van on Route 5, Maine — driver distracted by dog ("Bullet")
- 9+ fractures in right leg, 8 spine fractures, punctured lung, head wounds
- Near death. Returned to writing 5 weeks later.
- **Key line:** "The scariest moment is always just before you start."

## CRAFT (Lines 1100–2400)

### 1. Vocabulary — The First Word Test
- Take the first word that comes to mind if it's right and vivid
- "Rain" > "atmospheric precipitation"
- Don't reach for longer words out of shame
- **"Если хочешь, принимай во внимание уместность"** (George Carlin: prick/finger)
- Vocabulary lives in active use, not studying

### 2. Grammar
- Noun + verb = sentence
- **"Rocks detonate. Jane communicates. Mountains float."**
- Grammar is a pole to help thoughts walk
- When lost, return to simple noun-verb

### 3. Passive Voice = Cowardice
- Active: subject does the action
- Passive: subject suffers action
- "The meeting will be held at 7" → "The meeting is at 7"
- **"Passive voice is for writers who are afraid of their own words."**
- Passive = safe, like a passive lover. Avoid.

### 4. Adverbs = Road to Hell
- One adverb = dandelion on lawn (charming)
- Fifty = dead lawn
- **Kill adverbs. Especially in dialogue attribution.**
- "Said" is almost always sufficient
- **"Said-book"** — Larry McCarthy uses "said" exclusively even in emotional crises
- Swinburne test: "said" + adverb = swinburne, never use

### 5. Paragraphs
- Paragraph > sentence as basic unit
- Short paragraphs = easy read. Long = difficult material.
- One-sentence paragraph = speed change, conversational rhythm
- **"Paragraph is where writing starts to breathe."**
- The rhythm comes from thousands of hours of reading

### 6. Description
- Start with 4 concrete details that come to mind first
- "Палм-Ту" example: darkness/bar + sawdust + cartoons + smell of steaks
- More details ≠ better. "Modest dinner is better than feast."
- Let reader fill gaps. They're smart.
- Simile and metaphor — yes, but fresh. Avoid clichés.
- Best comparisons: Raymond Chandler, George V. Higgins

### 7. Dialogue
- Good dialogue = real exchange, not speeches
- **"Talk is characterization."**
- Realism > politeness. If character says " shit" when hit — write "shit."
- Lying to avoid profanity = breaking contract with reader
- **"If you want the dialogue to sound real, reproduce what people actually say when they hammer their thumb."**
- Elmore Leonard = master. H.P. Lovecraft = terrible.
- Listen more than you talk. IRL and in writing.

### 8. Characters
- Everyone considers themselves the main character, the protagonist
- **"Nobody in real life is 'the villain' — everyone is the hero of their own story."**
- Annie Wilkes (Misery): for herself, she's not crazy — she's a woman besieged
- **"Each character you create is partly yourself."**
- To understand a character: ask "what would I do?"

### 9. Story vs Plot
- **Story:** what happens (chronological)
- **Plot:** how it unfolds (what the reader experiences)
- Find the fossil in the ground — don't impose plot on story
- **"The book tells you what it wants to be."** (Alfred Bester)

### 10. Theme / Idea
- Don't start with theme. Start with story.
- After first draft: ask "what is this about, really?"
- Theme emerges from the work itself
- **Blood in Carrie** — not planned, discovered on reread
- Blood = menstruation, sacrifice, maturity, guilt, passing on traits

### 11. Rewriting
- **"Second draft = first draft minus 10%"**
- Close the door: write the first draft fast, enthusiastic
- Open the door: read after 6 weeks, make changes
- Tabitha = first reader. She is ruthless but right.
- Ideal Reader: write for one person
- Don't show work until first draft complete

### 12. Research
- Research = extended backstory
- Just enough detail to convince, not overwhelm
- "A pinch of credibility = spice in spaghetti"
- **"Don't let research become the tail wagging the dog."**

### 13. Finding an Agent / Publishing
- Read the market. Study Writer's Market.
- Write query letter: 6 published stories = credentials
- First rejection: form letter. Third rejection: personal note (rare).
- **"Good presentation"**: white paper, double-spaced, your address, word count
- Don't send to anyone who'll be offended by profanity if your characters curse

### 14. Writing Courses
- Can provide community, but:
  - "Workshop critique = vague"
  - "Every day with door open = premature optimization"
  - No magic bullet. Practice + reading = everything.
- **"Best writing lessons: teach yourself. Usually happen with door closed."**

## FINAL LESSONS

1. **"Write with the door closed, rewrite with it open."**
2. **2,000 words/day.** If nothing else — 1,000.
3. **Start before you're ready.** Scariest moment = just before starting.
4. **Life first.** Life is not support system for art.
5. **The book commands, the writer obeys.**
6. **Kill your darlings.** (Hemingway: "Kill your darlings.")
7. **Description = 4 details.** Readers fill the rest.
8. **Said is enough.** Never "said-book" yourself.
9. **No passive voice.** No adverbs in dialogue attribution.
10. **Read voraciously.** Bad books teach as much as good.
11. **Second draft = first draft minus 10%.**
12. **Write for one person.** (Ideal Reader = Tabby.)

## READING LIST (from King)

### Writing craft / influences on King
- Elmore Leonard (dialogue master)
- Raymond Chandler (description)
- Ross Macdonald (paragraph rhythm)
- George V. Higgins (dialogue, "The Friends of Eddie Coyle")
- James M. Cain (tough prose)

### Literary models
- Steinbeck ("The Grapes of Wrath")
- Hemingway (simple declarative sentences)
- T.S. Eliot
- William Carlos Williams
- Don DeLillo ("Underworld")

### His recommendations for writers
- Read broadly. Read bad books. Read good books.
- Know when to close the door.
- Know when to open it.
